## üîÑ CONVERSION ANALYSIS

### LLC ‚Üí S-Corp Conversion

**When to Convert:**
- Annual profit consistently >$60,000
- No outside investors (or <100 shareholders)
- Willing to run payroll
- Plan to stay profitable

**Conversion Process:**
1. **Elect S-Corp Status:** File Form 2553 by March 15
   - Must be filed within 2 months 15 days of year start
   - Or file for following year by Dec 31
2. **Set Up Payroll:** Register with IRS, state
   - Use Gusto, ADP, or similar
   - Run at least quarterly
3. **Determine Reasonable Salary:**
   - Research comparable positions
   - Industry standards: $60K-$100K typical for owner
   - Document rationale
4. **Establish Accounting:**
   - Separate salary from distributions
   - Track basis
   - File 1120-S annually

**Costs:**
- Formation/Conversion: $500-$1,500
- Payroll service: $40-$150/month
- CPA for 1120-S: $1,500-$3,000/year
- **Total Annual Cost:** $2,000-$5,000

**Break-Even Analysis:**
```
Annual S-Corp Cost: $3,000
SE Tax Savings: 15.3% √ó (Profit - Salary)

Break-Even = $3,000 √∑ 15.3% = $19,608
Minimum Profit for Savings: $60,000+
(Assuming $40K salary, $20K distribution)
```

### LLC ‚Üí C-Corp Conversion

**When to Convert:**
- Planning to raise venture capital
- Need QSBS eligibility for exit
- Profit >$250K
- Want maximum R&D credit utilization
- Planning IPO or large acquisition

**Conversion Process:**
1. **State Conversion:** File articles of conversion
2. **IRS Notification:** File Form 8832 (entity classification)
   - Must file within 75 days of conversion
3. **Establish Corporate Formalities:**
   - Bylaws
   - Board of Directors
   - Shareholder agreements
   - Stock issuance
4. **Set Up Payroll:** All employees (including owner) on W-2
5. **Implement Benefits:**
   - Health insurance (100% deductible)
   - Retirement matching
   - Other fringe benefits

**Costs:**
- Legal fees: $5,000-$15,000
- Formation: $1,000-$2,000
- Payroll: $100-$300/month
- CPA for 1120: $3,000-$6,000/year
- **Total Annual Cost:** $6,000-$15,000

**Tax Implications:**
- Corporate tax: 21% flat rate
- Dividend tax: 0%, 15%, or 20% (when distributed)
- Effective rate: 21% - 36.8% depending on distribution strategy

## üìã S-CORP REASONABLE SALARY GUIDELINES

### Factors to Consider:
1. **Time devoted to business** (full-time vs part-time)
2. **Duties and responsibilities** (CEO, developer, sales)
3. **Compensation of non-shareholder employees**
4. **Distributions and other payments to shareholders**
5. **Comparable salaries in similar businesses**

### Reasonable Salary Ranges (EdTech, 2026):

| Role | Low | Mid | High |
|------|-----|-----|------|
| **CEO/Founder** | $60,000 | $80,000 | $120,000 |

| **CTO** | $80,000 | $110,000 | $160,000 |
| **Combined Role (Founder/CTO)** | $70,000 | $90,000 | $130,000 |

### Salary vs Distribution Rule of Thumb:
- **Conservative:** 60-70% salary, 30-40% distribution
- **Aggressive:** 40-50% salary, 50-60% distribution
- **IRS Red Flag:** <40% salary

## üìä QSBS (QUALIFIED SMALL BUSINESS STOCK) ANALYSIS

### What is QSBS?
- Section 1202 of Internal Revenue Code
- Excludes up to $10M or 10x basis from capital gains
- Must be C-Corp stock held 5+ years

### Requirements:
1. **C-Corporation at issuance** (not S-Corp or LLC)
2. **Active business** (not holding company)
3. **Gross assets <$50M** at issuance
4. **Held 5+ years** by non-corporate taxpayer
5. **Qualified trade/business** (tech companies qualify)

### Benefit Example:
```
Exit Scenario:
Original Investment: $100,000
Exit Value: $5,000,000
Gain: $4,900,000

WITHOUT QSBS (LLC/S-Corp):
Capital Gains Tax (20%): $980,000
Net Proceeds: $3,920,000

WITH QSBS (C-Corp):
Excluded Gain: $4,900,000
Taxable Gain: $0
Tax Saved: $980,000
Net Proceeds: $4,900,000
```

### When QSBS Matters:
- **Planning exit in 5+ years:** Critical consideration
- **Expecting >$1M gain:** Worth C-Corp conversion
- **Raising venture capital:** Usually required by VCs
- **Current LLC/S-Corp:** Can convert to C-Corp (clock starts at conversion)

### Section 1045 Rollover:
- Sell QSBS before 5 years ‚Üí reinvest in new QSBS within 60 days
- Defer gain recognition
- New 5-year clock starts

## ‚ö†Ô∏è COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID

### S-Corp Mistakes:
1. **No salary:** IRS can reclassify all distributions as wages
2. **Unreasonable low salary:** Audit red flag
3. **Missing Form 2553 deadline:** Can't elect retroactively
4. **Not running payroll:** Penalties and interest
5. **Commingling funds:** Piercing corporate veil risk
6. **Missing basis tracking:** Tax reporting errors

### C-Corp Mistakes:
1. **Excessive compensation:** Reclassified as dividend
2. **Accumulated earnings tax:** >$250K retained without business purpose
3. **Personal use of corporate assets:** Taxable fringe benefit
4. **Missing corporate formalities:** Piercing veil risk
5. **Double taxation:** Withdrawing as dividend instead of salary

### LLC Mistakes:
1. **Not documenting decisions:** Piercing veil risk
2. **Ignoring operating agreement:** Disputes with partners
3. **Missing self-employment tax:** Underestimating tax burden
4. **No R&D documentation:** Missing credits

## üí° OPTIMIZATION STRATEGIES

### S-Corp Optimization:
1. **Maximize pre-salary deductions:**
   - Solo 401(k): $69,000/year
   - Health insurance: 100% deductible
   - HSA: $4,150 individual, $8,300 family

2. **Optimize salary timing:**
   - Lower salary early in year (if cash flow tight)
   - True up by year-end
   - Consistent monthly salary looks better to IRS

3. **Retained earnings strategy:**
   - Leave profits in business for growth
   - No additional tax until distributed
   - Build working capital

### C-Corp Optimization:
1. **Zero-dividend strategy:**
   - Pay only salary, retain all profits
   - Avoid double taxation
   - Reinvest in growth

2. **Fringe benefits:**
   - Health insurance (100% deductible)
   - Life insurance ($50K coverage tax-free)
   - Education assistance ($5,250 tax-free)
   - De minimis benefits

3. **R&D credit stacking:**
   - Offset 21% corporate tax
   - Carry forward 20 years
   - Can be worth 10-15% of R&D spend
