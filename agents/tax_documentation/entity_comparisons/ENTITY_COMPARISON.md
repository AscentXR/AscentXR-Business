## âœ… PROS & CONS

### LLC / Sole Proprietorship

**PROS:**
- Simplest structure
- Lowest formation/maintenance costs
- No payroll required
- Pass-through taxation (no double tax)
- Flexibility in profit distribution
- Easy to convert to S-Corp later

**CONS:**
- Highest self-employment tax (15.3% on ALL profit)
- No liability protection (sole prop)
- Limited credibility vs corporations
- No tax-free fringe benefits
- Self-employment tax caps at $176,100 income

**BEST FOR:**
- Startups with <$60K profit
- Testing business concept
- Maximum simplicity preference
- No immediate tax optimization need

---

### S-Corporation

**PROS:**
- SE tax savings (15.3% on salary only, not distributions)
- Liability protection
- Pass-through taxation (no double tax)
- Credibility with customers/partners
- QBI deduction eligible (20%)
- Can deduct health insurance

**CONS:**
- Must pay reasonable salary (IRS scrutiny)
- Payroll required (costs ~$1,500/year)
- More complex tax filings (1120-S, K-1s)
- Stricter ownership rules
- Harder to bring in investors
- Must distribute pro-rata by ownership

**BEST FOR:**
- Profitable businesses ($60K-$200K net)
- Service businesses with low overhead
- Owner-operators taking active role
- No outside investors planned

---

### C-Corporation

**PROS:**
- Lowest corporate tax rate (21% flat)
- Unlimited shareholders
- Any type of shareholder (individuals, entities, foreign)
- Multiple classes of stock
- Easier to raise capital
- **QSBS exclusion** (100% tax-free on first $10M gain)
- R&D credit directly offsets corporate tax
- Better fringe benefits

**CONS:**
- Double taxation (corp + personal)
- Most complex structure
- Higher compliance costs
- Requires formal board meetings
- Unfavorable for losses (trapped at corp level)
- Not eligible for QBI deduction

**BEST FOR:**
- Raising venture capital
- Planning IPO or major acquisition
- High-growth startups
- QSBS planning (5+ year hold)
- Businesses reinvesting profits

## ðŸ”„ CONVERSION PROCESS

### LLC â†’ S-Corp

**Timeline:**
1. **Before March 15** (for current year election)
2. File Form 2553 with IRS
3. Elect S-Corp status effective Jan 1
4. Set up payroll system (Gusto/ADP)
5. File first 1120-S by March 15 next year

**Steps:**
```
1. Determine reasonable salary
   â†’ Research industry standards
   â†’ Document methodology
   â†’ Typical: 40-60% of net profit

2. File Form 2553
   â†’ All shareholders must sign
   â†’ File within 2 months 15 days of year-start
   â†’ Or late election relief available

3. Set up payroll
   â†’ Choose provider (Gusto recommended)
   â†’ Run at least quarterly
   â†’ Withhold federal/state taxes
   â†’ File 941s quarterly, W-2s annually

4. Update accounting
   â†’ Separate owner salary from distributions
   â†’ Track basis (contributions + income - distributions)
   â†’ File 1120-S annually

5. Ongoing compliance
   â†’ Reasonable salary review annually
   â†’ Payroll tax deposits
   â†’ Corporate formalities (optional for single-owner)
```

### LLC â†’ C-Corp

**Timeline:**
1. Form new C-Corp entity
2. LLC contributes assets to C-Corp for stock
3. LLC distributes stock to members
4. LLC dissolves

**Tax Treatment:**
- Can be tax-free if done correctly (Section 351)
- Must meet control requirements (80%)
- Consult tax attorney

### S-Corp â†’ C-Corp

**Process:**
1. Revoke S-Corp election (voluntary or automatic)
2. File with IRS before year-end for next year effect
3. Convert to C-Corp status
4. Begin corporate tax filings

**Consequences:**
- Double taxation begins
- Can be done once every 5 years
- May trigger built-in gains tax if <5 years as S-Corp

## ðŸ“‹ SALARY REASONABLENESS GUIDELINES

### IRS Factors for "Reasonable Salary":
1. **Training and experience**
2. **Duties and responsibilities**
3. **Time and effort devoted**
4. **Dividend history**
5. **Payments to non-shareholder employees**
6. **Timing and manner of paying bonuses**
7. **Comparable salaries**
8. **Compensation agreements**

- Document decision process
- Review annually
- Adjust for business performance
- Keep records of methodology

## ðŸš¨ PITFALLS TO AVOID

### S-Corp Mistakes:
- [ ] **Zero salary:** IRS reclassifies distributions as salary + penalties
- [ ] **Unreasonably low salary:** Same as zero, but harder to defend
- [ ] **No payroll:** Must run payroll at least quarterly
- [ ] **Mixing personal/corp:** Maintain separate accounts
- [ ] **Missing deadlines:** File 2553 on time, 1120-S by March 15

### C-Corp Mistakes:
- [ ] **Accumulated earnings:** >$250K retained earnings can trigger penalty tax
- [ ] **Excessive compensation:** IRS may reclassify as dividend
- [ ] **Missing corporate formalities:** Board meetings, minutes required

### All Structures:
- [ ] **Commingling funds:** Keep business/personal separate
- [ ] **Missing estimated taxes:** Penalties + interest
- [ ] **Poor documentation:** Harder to defend in audit

## ðŸ’¡ OPTIMIZATION STRATEGIES

### For S-Corp Owners:
1. **Optimize salary:** Not too low (IRS risk), not too high (SE tax waste)
2. **Time distributions:** Year-end for cash flow
3. **Health insurance:** Deductible above-the-line
4. **Retirement:** Solo 401(k) contributions reduce W-2 income
5. **QBI deduction:** 20% of qualified business income

### For C-Corp Owners:
1. **QSBS planning:** Hold stock 5+ years for $10M exclusion
2. **Salary vs dividend:** Balance payroll tax vs double tax
3. **Fringe benefits:** Medical, life insurance tax-free to employee
4. **R&D credits:** Directly offset 21% corporate tax
5. **Accumulated earnings:** Plan distributions or justification
